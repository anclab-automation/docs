An ontology is a formal, machine-readable representation of knowledge within a specific domain. It defines the concepts that exist in that domain, the properties those concepts carry, and the relationships between them — giving both humans and software systems a shared, unambiguous vocabulary. By encoding this structure explicitly, you enable applications to reason over data, detect inconsistencies, and draw conclusions that go beyond what is directly stated.

## Core Components

Every ontology is built from four foundational building blocks. Understanding each one helps you design ontologies that are both expressive and maintainable.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Classes — Types of Entities">
    A **class** represents a category or type of thing in your domain. Think of classes as the nouns of your ontology. For example, in a biomedical ontology you might define `Disease`, `Drug`, and `Protein` as classes.

    Classes can be arranged in hierarchies. A `RareDisease` class can be declared a subclass of `Disease`, inheriting its properties while adding more specific constraints.

    ```turtle
    # Turtle (RDF/OWL) — class hierarchy
    :Disease       a owl:Class .
    :RareDisease   a owl:Class ;
                   rdfs:subClassOf :Disease .
    ```
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Properties — Attributes and Relationships">
    **Properties** describe what you can say about instances of a class. OWL distinguishes two kinds:

    - **Datatype properties** link an individual to a literal value (a string, number, or date).
    - **Object properties** link an individual to another individual, expressing relationships between entities.

    ```turtle
    # Datatype property
    :hasApprovalYear   a owl:DatatypeProperty ;
                       rdfs:domain :Drug ;
                       rdfs:range  xsd:gYear .

    # Object property
    :treats            a owl:ObjectProperty ;
                       rdfs:domain :Drug ;
                       rdfs:range  :Disease .
    ```
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Individuals — Instances of Classes">
    An **individual** (also called an instance) is a specific, named member of a class. Individuals are where your ontology connects to real-world data.

    ```turtle
    :Aspirin    a :Drug ;
                :hasApprovalYear "1899"^^xsd:gYear ;
                :treats          :Headache .

    :Headache   a :Disease .
    ```
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Axioms — Logical Constraints">
    **Axioms** are logical statements that constrain how classes and properties can be used. They allow a reasoner to validate your data and infer new facts automatically. Common axiom types include:

    - **Domain and range restrictions** — constrain which classes a property applies to.
    - **Cardinality restrictions** — require exactly one, at least one, or at most N values.
    - **Disjointness** — declare that two classes cannot share members.
    - **Equivalence** — state that two classes or individuals are the same.

    ```turtle
    # Cardinality: a Drug must treat at least one Disease
    :Drug   rdfs:subClassOf [
              a owl:Restriction ;
              owl:onProperty    :treats ;
              owl:minCardinality 1
            ] .

    # Disjointness: Drug and Disease share no members
    :Drug   owl:disjointWith :Disease .
    ```
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## A Minimal Ontology Example

The snippet below brings all four components together in a single, self-contained Turtle file. You can load this directly into any OWL-compatible triplestore or ontology editor such as Protégé.

```turtle
@prefix :      <https://example.org/pharma#> .
@prefix owl:   <http://www.w3.org/2002/07/owl#> .
@prefix rdf:   <http://www.w3.org/1999/02/22-rdf-syntax-ns#> .
@prefix rdfs:  <http://www.w3.org/2000/01/rdf-schema#> .
@prefix xsd:   <http://www.w3.org/2001/XMLSchema#> .

# ── Ontology declaration ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
<https://example.org/pharma> a owl:Ontology ;
    rdfs:label "Minimal Pharmaceutical Ontology"@en .

# ── Classes ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
:Substance   a owl:Class .
:Drug        a owl:Class ; rdfs:subClassOf :Substance .
:Disease     a owl:Class .

:Drug owl:disjointWith :Disease .

# ── Properties ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
:treats      a owl:ObjectProperty ;
             rdfs:domain :Drug ;
             rdfs:range  :Disease .

:activeIngredient  a owl:DatatypeProperty ;
                   rdfs:domain :Drug ;
                   rdfs:range  xsd:string .

# ── Individuals ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
:Ibuprofen   a :Drug ;
             :activeIngredient "ibuprofen" ;
             :treats :Inflammation .

:Inflammation a :Disease ;
              rdfs:label "Inflammation"@en .
```

## OWL and SKOS

Two W3C standards dominate ontology engineering. Choosing between them depends on how much formal reasoning power you need.

### OWL — Web Ontology Language

OWL is a full description-logic language. It supports class expressions, property characteristics (transitivity, symmetry, inverse), and cardinality restrictions. A reasoner such as HermiT or Pellet can automatically classify your hierarchy and detect logical contradictions.

Use OWL when:
- You need automated classification or consistency checking.
- Your domain has complex logical relationships (e.g., "a parent is someone who has at least one child").
- You are integrating heterogeneous datasets and need equivalence mappings.

### SKOS — Simple Knowledge Organization System

SKOS models concept schemes: thesauri, taxonomies, and controlled vocabularies. It is deliberately lightweight — there is no formal reasoning, but it is easy to publish and link.

```turtle
@prefix skos: <http://www.w3.org/2004/02/skos/core#> .
@prefix :     <https://example.org/taxonomy#> .

:Analytics   a skos:Concept ;
             skos:prefLabel    "Analytics"@en ;
             skos:narrower     :GraphAnalytics ;
             skos:narrower     :PredictiveAnalytics .

:GraphAnalytics a skos:Concept ;
             skos:prefLabel    "Graph Analytics"@en ;
             skos:broader      :Analytics .
```

<Tip>
  Use **OWL** when you need a reasoner to enforce constraints and infer new class memberships. Use **SKOS** when you are publishing a controlled vocabulary or thesaurus and interoperability across catalogues matters more than logical expressivity. You can always combine both: define your formal class hierarchy in OWL and attach SKOS labels and mappings to the same URIs.
</Tip>

## Ontology Types

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Upper Ontologies">
    An **upper ontology** (also called a foundational or top-level ontology) defines the most general concepts that apply across all domains — things like `Object`, `Event`, `Process`, `Quality`, and `Time`. Examples include BFO (Basic Formal Ontology), DOLCE, and SUMO.

    By grounding your domain ontology in an upper ontology, you gain a principled framework for cross-domain integration and interoperability.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Domain Ontologies">
    A **domain ontology** models concepts within a specific field of knowledge: medicine, law, finance, manufacturing, or ecology. It is typically aligned to an upper ontology and may import or extend other domain ontologies.

    Well-known examples include SNOMED CT (clinical medicine), Gene Ontology (molecular biology), and FIBO (financial industry).
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Application Ontologies">
    An **application ontology** is purpose-built for a specific software system or use case. It may reuse classes from domain ontologies while adding constraints or extensions that only make sense within that application's context.

    Application ontologies trade generality for precision: they are optimised for a particular task and are often not intended for reuse outside that context.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## SHACL — Shape Constraints for Data Validation

SHACL (Shapes Constraint Language) lets you define data quality rules that operate on your RDF graph independently of OWL reasoning. Where OWL axioms drive inference, SHACL shapes drive **validation** — you specify what well-formed data must look like and then check whether your graph satisfies those shapes.

```turtle
@prefix sh:   <http://www.w3.org/ns/shacl#> .
@prefix :     <https://example.org/pharma#> .
@prefix xsd:  <http://www.w3.org/2001/XMLSchema#> .

:DrugShape  a sh:NodeShape ;
            sh:targetClass :Drug ;
            sh:property [
              sh:path        :activeIngredient ;
              sh:datatype    xsd:string ;
              sh:minCount    1 ;
              sh:maxCount    1 ;
              sh:message     "Every Drug must have exactly one activeIngredient."
            ] ;
            sh:property [
              sh:path     :treats ;
              sh:class    :Disease ;
              sh:minCount 1 ;
              sh:message  "Every Drug must treat at least one Disease."
            ] .
```

<Note>
  SHACL and OWL serve complementary roles. Run OWL reasoning first to materialise inferred triples, then apply SHACL shapes to validate that the enriched graph meets your data-quality requirements.
</Note>
